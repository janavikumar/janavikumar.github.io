Ocean & Climate — A Scrollytelling Primer

The Pulse of the Pacific

Every two to seven years, a patch of ocean roughly the size of the continental United States warms by a degree or two — and weather changes on almost every continent on Earth.

This is El Niño. Understanding it starts with a single, unglamorous number, and ends somewhere much bigger: the food on your plate, the storms on your coast, and a planet where this signal is only getting louder.

The Measurement

The Box That Forecasts a Planet

+0.5°C
the threshold, sustained for five overlapping three-month periods

Scientists watch one rectangle of ocean above all others: the Niño 3.4 region, a stretch of the central and eastern tropical Pacific. When its sea surface temperature climbs at least half a degree Celsius above normal — and stays there — forecasters start paying very close attention.

Temperature alone isn't enough to declare an El Niño, though. The atmosphere has to respond in kind: trade winds must weaken, and rainfall has to shift eastward across the Pacific. It's only when ocean and atmosphere move together that El Niño is officially underway.

Three Faces of One Ocean

El Niño, La Niña, and the In-Between

El Niño is the warm phase of a larger cycle called ENSO — the El Niño-Southern Oscillation. Its cold counterpart, La Niña, cools the same waters and flips many of the same weather patterns in reverse. Between the two sits a neutral phase, when the tropical Pacific runs close to its long-term average.

Spanish-speaking fishermen off Peru and Ecuador noticed the warm phase centuries ago, and gave it a name that has stuck ever since: El Niño, the Christ Child, for its habit of arriving around Christmas.

How the Signal Travels

Teleconnections: A Warm Patch Rewires the Sky

Here is the part that surprises people: El Niño's biggest effects often aren't felt anywhere near the tropical Pacific. As trade winds weaken and warm water sloshes eastward, heat and moisture pour into the atmosphere above it — and that extra energy shifts the position of the jet stream thousands of miles away.

Meteorologists call these long-distance links teleconnections. A strong El Niño can steer more winter storms toward California and the Gulf Coast, fuel record rains in Peru and Chile, and simultaneously starve Australia, Indonesia, and parts of southern Asia of rain. One ocean, many weather systems, wired together.

A Changing Signal

A Louder Signal in a Warmer World

~13%
projected increase in ENSO's global sea-surface-temperature influence under greenhouse warming

Recent climate modeling, published in Nature Communications, suggests El Niño's global reach is set to grow. Across a large ensemble of climate simulations, researchers found that as the planet warms, El Niño's fingerprint on sea surface temperatures strengthens almost everywhere on the map — not just near the tropical Pacific.

Two mechanisms seem to drive it: stronger El Niño-related winds, which intensify the atmospheric teleconnections that carry the signal outward, and a growing difference in moisture between the ocean surface and the air above it, which amplifies evaporation and heat exchange. In plain terms — as the background climate warms, the same El Niño produces a bigger ripple than it used to.

Not a New Story

A Pattern Written Into History

Teleconnections aren't a modern discovery — they've been shaping human life for centuries, often invisibly. In my own research on early modern Europe, I found that El Niño events were linked to more than 40% of documented famine onsets in Central Europe between 1500 and 1800, as the same wind and rainfall shifts described above drove wetter summers and failed harvests thousands of miles from the Pacific.

Grain prices in the study rose an average of 6.5% in the year following an El Niño — and the price shock spread even into regions with no direct climate impact, as strained markets pushed costs outward. It's a reminder that ENSO's reach isn't just a meteorological curiosity. It has been quietly shaping harvests, prices, and human history for as long as we've kept records.

The Takeaway

One Ocean, Every Coastline

A single patch of the tropical Pacific, watched through one number in one small box on a map, shapes droughts, floods, harvests, and fisheries around the globe — and as the planet warms, that reach appears to be growing stronger, not weaker.
